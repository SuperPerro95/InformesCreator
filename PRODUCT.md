# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Argentine primary and secondary school teachers who set aside time on weekends or planning periods to write student progress reports. They work methodically, often on their personal laptop at home, sometimes at a school desk during quieter hours. They take pride in their writing and want each report to read as carefully as they would write it by hand, but without the tedium of drafting 25 from scratch.

They are moderately tech-literate, comfortable with word processors and web apps but not developers. They value tools that respect their time without making them feel rushed. Their emotional state when using this tool is focused and deliberate, seeking quality over speed.

## Product Purpose

InformesCreator uses local AI (Ollama) to turn structured classroom observations into polished, professional-grade student progress reports. It exists because writing 30 individual reports well demands more evening and weekend hours than teachers have.

Success looks like a teacher producing reports that a principal or parent would read without questioning the quality. Each report sounds like the teacher's own voice, carries the right level of detail, and reflects the care they put into their work. The tool disappears into the task.

## Brand Personality

Patient, organized, elegant

A precise instrument, not a frantic assistant. The interface rewards a deliberate pace with professional output. Every screen feels like a well-designed tool: nothing is where you don't expect it, nothing takes more than one click to find, nothing demands attention that hasn't earned it.

The personality is confident but quiet, like an architect's drafting table or a leather-bound grade book. It respects the teacher's craft by never getting in the way.

## Anti-references

Not a bureaucratic form. Not a complicated government portal with multi-panel layouts, tiny gray labels, and instructions you need to read twice. Not childhood education software with cartoon mascots, bright primary colors, and gamified badges. Not a generic AI chatbot UI with typing indicators and neon accents.

The interface should feel more like a well-organized document editor than a dashboard. The teacher should never have to wonder where the next action is.

## Design Principles

1. **Craft over speed.** The interface communicates that the output matters. Transitions are smooth, spacing is generous, typography is deliberate. Nothing looks rushed.
2. **Focused surfaces, not dashboards.** Every screen has one job. The wizard shows one question at a time. The course view surfaces the student list and nothing else. If a screen has more than five interactive elements, it needs simplification.
3. **Professional without being cold.** Clean lines, restrained color, generous whitespace. A school principal could see the screen over the teacher's shoulder and take the tool seriously. Warmth lives in the materials (paper-like backgrounds, blue ink accents), not in decorative gestures.
4. **Predictable interaction model.** Buttons do what they say. The wizard progresses forward with clear steps. Nothing resets, reorders, or disappears without explanation. The tool earns trust by being consistent.
5. **阿根廷 context, always.** Spanish-language UI with Argentine phrasing. School terminology follows local usage (ESN5, primaria/secundaria, trimestre). Internationalized idioms are replaced with native equivalents, never translated literally.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA compliance. Sufficient contrast ratios across both light and dark palettes. Full keyboard navigation for wizard, drawer, and form interactions. Screen reader labels on every interactive element, including icon-only buttons. Respects `prefers-reduced-motion`. Color is never the sole indicator of state; icons and text labels accompany every status change.
